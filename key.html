Keyboard Instruments



About

From the earliest organs to the modern piano, keyboard instruments can produce a great variety of sound. With their wide range of notes, keyboards can match a whole orchestra but they have also inspired composers to write the most intimate solo pieces. The piano has become an icon of Western music and no other instrument has proved as versatile or as influential.

Piano

The piano has the widest range of notes of any instrument, covering more than seven octaves. The word "piano" is short for pianoforte, meaning that the instrument can be played softer (what a musician would call "piano") or louder (known as "forte") in response to the player's touch on the keyboard. When a key is pressed on the keyboard, it moves a felt-covered hammer that strikes a steel string, making it vibrate to produce a sound. The piano is a versatile instrument and is played in all types of music, including classical, popular, and jazz.

Harpsichord

The harpsichord was a popular solo and accompanying instrument in Europe in the 16th and 17th centuries (the Baroque period), after which the piano took its place. The strings of harpsichord are plucked, unlike piano strings that are hit with small hammers. When a key is pressed down a small piece of quill or plastic (the plectrum) plucks a string to sound the note. The note is then "damped" or stopped by a piece of cloth or felt when the key is released. Unlike the piano, the notes of a harpsichord are sounded at the same volume however lightly or heavily a key is pressed.

Organ

The organ is the erliest keyboard instrument. Used in churches for centuries, it can also be played as a concert instrument in both classical and popular music. It works in a similar way to woodwind and brass instruments. Air is blown to produce sound. There are three main parts of an organ: the pipes, the bellows, and the controls. The pipes differ in size and shape to make different sounds. The keyboard controls different rows of pipes and each pipe produces a different note.
